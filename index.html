Step 02
DATA CLEANING

Before drawing any conclusions, we fixed four major data quality issues. Here's exactly what we did and why each step mattered.

4-Step Cleaning Process
1
Extract the number from messy text
A pattern-matching rule (regex) pulls the first number from any string. '39 PS @ 11000 rpm' becomes 39.
2
Convert European PS units to HP
1 PS = 0.9863 HP. We detected PS values and applied the conversion so every bike uses the same unit.
3
Calculate the Efficiency score
Efficiency = HP ÷ CC. This normalises power across engine sizes — the heart of our analysis.
4
Remove impossible values
Real bikes score 0.05–0.25 HP/CC. One BMW showed 140 HP/CC — a clear typo. Removed before all calculations.
⚠ Critical Outlier Removed
BMW record with Efficiency = 140 HP/CC — physically impossible (best real bikes ~0.22). Data entry error. Removed to protect result integrity.
What "Efficiency" Actually Means
HP ÷ CC
Horsepower divided by Engine Size
Big bike
200HP ÷ 999cc
= 0.200
Small bike
39HP ÷ 296cc
= 0.132
💡 Why not just use raw HP? HP/CC reveals engineering skill — not just engine size. A tuned 400cc engine can outscore a lazy 1000cc engine.

Step 05
THE FINDINGS

We set three research questions before starting. Here's what the data answered — in plain English, with the charts to prove it.

Question 01
Does Kawasaki make the most efficient small bikes (under 400cc)?

Yes. Kawasaki's Ninja 300 and Ninja 400 score 0.122 HP per CC — the highest of any brand below 400cc. Kawasaki deliberately over-engineers their entry bikes so the "Ninja" name carries real performance credibility at budget prices.

✓ CONFIRMED — Kawasaki #1 in small bikes
Small Bike Rankings
Question 02
Do bigger engines become less efficient per cc?

Yes — the diminishing returns effect is real. Small 300cc bikes average 0.148 HP/CC. Scale up to 1000cc and the average falls to 0.132 HP/CC. Massive engines prioritise torque over efficiency per cc. Correlation r = −0.32 confirms this across hundreds of models.

✓ CONFIRMED — r = −0.32 negative trend
Efficiency by Engine Class
Question 03
How different are the top 1000cc bikes across brands in 2026?

Barely different. Yamaha and Kawasaki tie at ~0.164 HP/CC, separated by just 0.0002. The #1-to-#5 gap is only 0.012. Modern engineering has hit a ceiling — all five brands are extracting near-maximum efficiency from similar engines.

~ NEAR PARITY — 0.012 gap across 5 brands
